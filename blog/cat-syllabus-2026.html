CAT Syllabus 2026: Section-Wise Topics, Weightage & Preparation Plan

Updated July 2026 · 8 min read

The CAT exam doesn't have an officially published syllabus. The IIMs never release a fixed topic list — what you see below is compiled from 10+ years of previous CAT papers and consistent year-on-year trends. That's actually good news: it means the syllabus is predictable, even if it isn't official.

CAT is split into three sections — VARC (Verbal Ability & Reading Comprehension), DILR (Data Interpretation & Logical Reasoning), and QA (Quantitative Aptitude). Each section gets a fixed 40-minute window, and you cannot switch between sections once the timer starts.

VARC Syllabus & Weightage

VARC has two parts — Reading Comprehension (RC) and Verbal Ability (VA). RC alone makes up nearly two-thirds of the section, usually spread across 4-5 passages of 400-600 words each, covering analytical, descriptive, and data-driven topics.

  • Reading Comprehension — 4-5 passages, roughly 16 questions
  • Para-jumbles — arranging sentences in logical order
  • Para-summary — picking the best one-line summary of a paragraph
  • Odd sentence out — identifying the sentence that doesn't belong

There's no direct vocabulary or grammar testing anymore — everything is passage or paragraph-based, which is why daily reading habit matters more than memorising word lists.

DILR Syllabus & Weightage

DILR is widely considered the most unpredictable section — the format of individual sets can change from year to year, but the broad categories stay consistent:

  • Arrangements (linear, circular, matrix-based) and seating puzzles
  • Data tables, bar graphs, line graphs, and pie charts
  • Games & tournaments, scheduling problems
  • Venn diagrams, network/route-based sets
  • Cubes and other 3D reasoning sets

DILR usually comes as sets of 4-6 questions built around one dataset — solve one set fully rather than half-solving several, since accuracy on a chosen set matters more than attempting everything.

QA Syllabus & Weightage

QA is the most syllabus-driven of the three sections — Arithmetic and Algebra alone account for a large share of questions most years.

  • Arithmetic (highest weightage) — Percentages, Profit & Loss, Time-Speed-Distance, Time & Work, Ratios, Averages, Mixtures
  • Algebra — Linear & Quadratic Equations, Functions, Inequalities, Logarithms
  • Geometry & Mensuration — Triangles, Circles, Coordinate Geometry, 2D/3D Mensuration
  • Number Systems — Divisibility, Remainders, Factors, HCF-LCM
  • Modern Maths — Permutation & Combination, Probability, Set Theory, Sequences & Series

If your basics in Arithmetic are strong, QA becomes the most scorable section on the entire paper — it rewards preparation more directly than VARC or DILR do.

How to Prioritise Your Prep

With a fixed syllabus but no official weightage document, the smartest approach is to prepare in order of what shows up most consistently:

  1. Start with Arithmetic (QA) — it has the single highest weightage across all three sections combined.
  2. Build a daily RC habit — read one long-form article or editorial every day, minimum 2 passages.
  3. Practice one DILR set daily from previous years' papers (2017 onwards) to get comfortable with varying formats.
  4. Take sectional mocks before jumping into full-length tests, so you can isolate weak areas early.
  5. Move to full mocks once each section feels manageable individually — aim for at least 20-25 full-length mocks before exam day.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does CAT have an official syllabus?

No. IIMs do not publish an official syllabus. This guide is based on consistent patterns from previous years' CAT papers.

Which section has the highest weightage in QA?

Arithmetic — it typically accounts for the largest share of QA questions, often 35-40% of the section.

How long does it take to cover the full CAT syllabus?

Most aspirants take 6-9 months with consistent daily practice, though it varies based on your starting level in each section.

Is CAT syllabus the same as XAT or SNAP?

Broadly similar in structure (verbal, quant, reasoning) but the difficulty, question style, and section weightage differ. See our SNAP vs CAT comparison for details (coming soon).
